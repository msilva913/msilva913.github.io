Influences

Papers that shaped the research agenda

Several works have been formative in crafting my perspective and research agenda in economics. I list a select few below.

  • Bai, Y., Ríos-Rull, J.-V., and Storesletten, K. (2025). Demand shocks as technology shocks. Review of Economic Studies, rdaf045.
    Goods market search frictions give demand a productive role: higher consumer search raises match rates, output, and measured TFP, so preference shocks mimic technology shocks in the data.
  • Kiyotaki, N. and Moore, J. (1997). Credit cycles. Journal of Political Economy, 105(2), 211–248.
    Collateral constraints tie borrowing to asset values, so a small shock to asset prices tightens credit, depresses investment, and feeds back into further price declines — a powerful endogenous amplifier.
  • Cooper, R. and John, A. (1988). Coordinating coordination failures in Keynesian models. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 103(3), 441–463.
    When firms' payoffs are strategic complements, the economy can get stuck in low-activity traps even without any fundamental shock — a clean microfoundation for Keynesian demand failures.
  • Rocheteau, G., Wright, R., and Zhang, C. (2018). Corporate finance and monetary policy. American Economic Review, 108(4–5), 1147–1186.
    Firms that face liquidity constraints hold money as a buffer, linking the stock of real balances directly to investment — and giving monetary policy a real channel that bypasses the standard interest-rate mechanism.
  • Geromichalos, A. and Herrenbrueck, L. (2022). The liquidity-augmented model of macroeconomic aggregates: A New Monetarist DSGE approach. Review of Economic Dynamics, 45, 134–167.
    Assets are liquid not because they circulate as money, but because agents can sell them for money when liquidity needs arise — an "indirect liquidity" channel that reshapes how open market operations transmit to output and investment.
  • Guerrieri, V., Lorenzoni, G., Straub, L., and Werning, I. (2022). Macroeconomic implications of COVID-19: Can negative supply shocks cause demand shortages? American Economic Review, 112(5), 1437–1474.
    A supply disruption in one sector can propagate into a demand shortfall economy-wide when markets are incomplete and workers cannot fully smooth income — supply shocks become Keynesian recessions.
  • Mian, A., Rao, K., and Sufi, A. (2013). Household balance sheets, consumption, and the economic slump. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 128(4), 1687–1726.
    Counties where households were most leveraged saw the sharpest consumption drops after house prices fell, pinning the Great Recession's severity on household debt rather than financial-sector dysfunction alone.
  • Bilbiie, F.O., Ghironi, F., and Melitz, M.J. (2012). Endogenous entry, product variety, and business cycles. Journal of Political Economy, 120(2), 304–345.
    When product variety is endogenous, firm entry acts as a margin of adjustment that amplifies booms and deepens downturns, generating richer co-movement than standard RBC models.
  • Lagos, R. and Wright, R. (2005). A unified framework for monetary theory and policy analysis. Journal of Political Economy, 113(3), 463–484.
    By alternating decentralized trade with a competitive round that resets money holdings, Lagos and Wright make divisible money tractable and open New Monetarist theory to quantitative work.
  • Mortensen, D.T. and Pissarides, C.A. (1994). Job creation and job destruction in the theory of unemployment. Review of Economic Studies, 61(3), 397–415.
    The matching function and endogenous job destruction give unemployment a structural home: vacancies and job-seekers meet randomly, and firms destroy jobs whenever productivity falls below an endogenous threshold.
  • Aiyagari, S.R. (1994). Uninsured idiosyncratic risk and aggregate saving. Quarterly Journal of Economics, 109(3), 659–684.
    Without complete insurance, households self-insure by over-saving relative to the first best — raising the equilibrium interest rate and showing that heterogeneity has aggregate consequences even in a representative-firm economy.
  • Bernanke, B.S., Gertler, M., and Gilchrist, S. (1999). The financial accelerator in a quantitative business cycle framework. In Handbook of Macroeconomics, Vol. 1. Elsevier.
    Agency costs between borrowers and lenders create a wedge that rises in recessions, amplifying downturns through the balance-sheet channel and giving financial frictions quantitative bite in business cycle models.
