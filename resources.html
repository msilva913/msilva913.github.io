Resources

Papers · Data · Computation · Writing · AI

A curated reference list for researchers and students in macroeconomics and related fields. It covers data sources, computational tools, and writing guides I return to regularly.

Key Papers

  • Corporate Finance and Monetary Policy Rocheteau, Wright, and Zhang (AER, 2018). Firms facing liquidity constraints hold money as a productive buffer, so monetary policy transmits to investment directly through real balances — not just via the interest rate. A foundational New Monetarist treatment of the corporate finance channel.
  • Credit Crises, Precautionary Savings, and the Liquidity Trap Guerrieri and Lorenzoni (QJE, 2017). A tightening in borrowing limits forces constrained households to deleverage while unconstrained ones raise precautionary savings, depressing interest rates and generating an output drop even with flexible prices — and a deeper one when the zero lower bound binds.

Writing

  • Elements of Style William Strunk Jr. and E.B. White. The standard reference for clear, concise English prose.

Data

  • Current Population Survey (CPS) Primary source of U.S. labor force statistics. Administered by the Census Bureau under the Bureau of Labor Statistics.
  • Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP) Short panel covering employment status, earnings, income, and demographics at monthly frequency.
  • Consumer Expenditure Survey (CEX) Continuous data on U.S. consumer purchasing habits; consists of a quarterly interview panel and a diary component.
  • Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF) Triennial household survey covering balance sheets, pensions, income, and demographic characteristics.
  • Compustat (via WRDS) Balance sheet variables for publicly traded firms. Available through Wharton Research Data Services; subscription required.
  • American Time Use Survey (ATUS) Nationally representative U.S. time-diary survey. Extract builder available via IPUMS.
  • Utilization-Adjusted TFP Real-time quarterly TFP series adjusted for utilization, following Basu, Fernald, and Kimball (2006). San Francisco Fed.
  • Composite Help-Wanted Index A composite vacancy index developed by Regis Barnichon.
  • Business Dynamics Statistics (BDS) Annual establishment-level data on gross job creation and destruction starting in 1976, derived from the Longitudinal Business Database.
  • Business Employment Dynamics (BED) Quarterly BLS series on gross job gains and losses at the establishment and firm level, derived from the Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages. Covers openings, closings, expansions, and contractions from 1992 forward, broken down by industry, firm size, and state. Essential for studying the creative destruction margin of labor market dynamics.

Computation — Macroeconomics

  • QuantEcon Open-source libraries and lectures on quantitative economics in Python and Julia, by Thomas J. Sargent and John Stachurski.
  • Notes on Quantitative Macroeconomics Gianluca Violante. Comprehensive lecture notes covering modern quantitative macro methods.
  • Heterogeneous-Agent Macroeconomics Computational notes by Alisdair McKay. Covers Aiyagari, Krusell–Smith, and related models with Julia/Python code.
  • Bayesian Estimation of DSGE Models Slides and code by Herbst and Schorfheide.
  • GMM Notes Notes and examples on Generalized Method of Moments by Richard W. Evans.

Computation — Julia

  • Quantitative Economics with Julia Sargent and Stachurski via QuantEcon. The standard entry point for Julia applied to economics.
  • Numerical Analysis with Julia Giray Okten. Covers root-finding, interpolation, numerical integration, and ODEs.
  • From Zero to Julia Blog series by Aurelio Amerio. Particularly useful for researchers coming from Python or MATLAB.
  • Official Julia Documentation The definitive language reference at docs.julialang.org.
  • Official Julia Tutorial (YouTube) Video tutorials from the Julia Language channel.

Computation — Python

  • Quantitative Economics with Python Sargent and Stachurski via QuantEcon. Parallel to the Julia lectures; covers the full stack for quantitative macro.
  • Google's Python Class Free course from Google for people with some programming background.
  • Real Python Video lectures and tutorials on Python, from beginner through advanced topics.
  • Pandas Cookbook Recipes for scientific computing with Pandas. Practical reference for data wrangling.
  • 7 Python Tricks Practical tips for writing cleaner and more efficient Python code.
  • Speed Up Pandas with Dask or Swifter How to parallelize apply operations on large DataFrames.
  • DataCamp Interactive courses on Python, R, and data science tools.

AI-Assisted Research and Coding

  • Claude (Anthropic) My primary AI assistant for research and coding tasks. Particularly useful for drafting and editing prose, debugging Julia and Python code, working through model derivations, and building this website. Claude's ability to reason carefully about economics and handle long technical documents makes it well-suited for research work.
  • VS Code with GitHub Copilot VS Code is my preferred editor for Julia and Python; with GitHub Copilot integrated, it offers inline code suggestions that meaningfully speed up writing numerical routines, data pipelines, and simulation code. Especially effective for boilerplate-heavy tasks like setting up solvers or formatting output.
